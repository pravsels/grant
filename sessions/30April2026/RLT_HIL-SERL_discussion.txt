$$Target = \text{Reward} + \gamma \cdot Q(S_{next}, \pi(S_{next}))$$

Notice that the next action is **$\pi(S_{next})$** (what the *current Actor* would do), not the action recorded in the data.

* $\gamma = 0.9$
* The Actor is currently dumb: it always chooses the action **"Go Left"**.

2. Human intervenes at **$S_{ditch}$**. Human chooses **"Go Right"**. The robot moves to **$S_{goal}$**. Reward = 1 (Terminal).

Your Replay Buffer now has exactly two rows:
* **Row 1:** `(State: S_0, Action: Left, Reward: 0, Next State: S_ditch)`
* **Row 2:** `(State: S_ditch, Action: Right, Reward: 1, Next State: S_goal)`

**Step 1: The algorithm randomly samples Row 2 to train the Critic.**
Let's calculate the Target for the human's action.
$$Target = \text{Reward} + \gamma \cdot Q(S_{goal}, \pi(S_{goal}))$$
Because $S_{goal}$ is the end of the episode, the future Q-value is 0.
$$Target = 1 + 0 = 1$$
*Result:* The Critic learns that $Q(S_{ditch}, \text{Right}) = 1$. The human's action is correctly graded as great.

**Step 2: The algorithm randomly samples Row 1 to train the Critic.**

$$Target = \text{Reward} + \gamma \cdot Q(S_{ditch}, \pi(S_{ditch}))$$

To see why we need it, look at the structure of the TD update: you are updating a guess based on another guess.

For the sake of our thought experiment, you can treat them as containing the same knowledge. The critical part of the target equation is *which action* gets passed into that network.

The human data acts as the solid ground that the Critic and Actor use to build a staircase backward from the goal.

You are 100% mathematically correct. Once the Actor learns how to get out of the ditch, the value of the ditch *does* become high. The target for `(s_0, a_bad)` *will* become a high number. `Q(s_0, a_bad)` will no longer be zero.

So, why doesn't the robot intentionally drive into the ditch? 

The answer is the discount factor: **$\gamma$**.

Think of $\gamma$ (usually 0.99) as a **time tax**. Every step you take costs you a percentage of your final reward. 

Let's compare the two paths from $s_0$:

**Path 1: The Good Action**
Actor takes `a_good`. It stays on the center path. It is exactly 5 steps away from the goal.
The score for `Q(s_0, a_good)` will be $1.0 \cdot \gamma^5 \approx 0.95$.

**Path 2: The Ditch (The detour)**
Actor takes `a_bad`. It goes into the ditch. Because it's in the ditch, it has to take 10 extra steps to recover, zig-zag back to the center, and finally reach the goal. It is 15 steps away from the goal.
The score for `Q(s_0, a_bad)` will be $1.0 \cdot \gamma^{15} \approx 0.86$.

Because the Actor's only rule is to output the action with the *highest possible* Q-value, it will compare $0.95$ and $0.86$, and it will choose `a_good`. It will not purposely drive into the ditch.

But look at what you just uncovered. By learning to recover from the ditch, $Q(s_0, a_{bad})$ changed from $0$ to $0.86$. 

This isn't a flaw; this is the exact reason RL policies become incredibly **robust**. 
The robot doesn't *want* to go in the ditch ($0.95 > 0.86$), but if a human shoves the robot into the ditch, the robot doesn't panic. It knows exactly how to get out.

Do you see how we never looked more than 1 step into the future? But because the *next* state had already been updated by its own 1-step calculation, the $\gamma$ compounds.

If it takes 15 steps to reach the goal from the ditch, fifteen separate 1-step updates have to chain together. The math becomes $\gamma^{15}$.

Now that the 1-step mechanics are rock solid, we can finally look at the second paper: **RL Token (RLT)**.

We agreed earlier that the 1-step update creates a brutal bottleneck. If a task takes 500 steps, that $\gamma$ multiplier hits 500 times. The signal practically vanishes before it reaches the start of the task. 

RLT solves this by modifying the off-policy update with **Action Chunking** (Equation 3). Instead of taking 1 step and then asking the target network for the future value, the algorithm executes a "chunk" of $C$ steps all at once, adds up the immediate rewards for that whole chunk, and *then* asks the target network for the future value. 

If $C=10$, a 500-step task is suddenly compressed into 50 chunks. The "time tax" is only applied 50 times ($\gamma^{50}$), so the reward flows backward 10 times faster.

2. **Catastrophic Forgetting:** If you aggressively fine-tune the entire VLA to insert a screw, it will overwrite its prior knowledge and completely forget how to fold laundry. You ruin its generalization.

So, the RLT paper introduces a brilliant compromise. 

They **freeze** the giant VLA. They force it to compress all its rich visual and semantic understanding into a single, tiny, frozen vector: the **RL Token**. 

Then, they just slap a tiny, lightweight Actor-Critic network (exactly like the ones in HIL-SERL) on top of that RL Token. The tiny network trains blazingly fast, but it gets to look at the world through the "eyes" of a massive foundation model.
