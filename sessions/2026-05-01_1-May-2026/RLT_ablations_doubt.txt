Grant

This is a very natural intuition. It feels like both ablations are just different ways of saying "stop using the VLA." 

But mathematically, they are cutting two completely different wires. 

The confusion comes from the fact that the VLA reference action ($\mathbf{\tilde{a}}$) actually lives in two separate places in the math:

1. **The Information Wire (The Input):** $\pi_\theta(\cdot \mid \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{\tilde{a}})$. The actor gets to *see* the VLA's suggestion on the forward pass before making a decision.
2. **The Gravity Wire (The Loss):** $\dots + \beta \| \mathbf{a} - \mathbf{\tilde{a}} \|_2^2$. The actor gets *penalized* during the backward pass if its output strays too far from the VLA's suggestion.

Here is what happens when you sever them one at a time:

**"w/o pass-through" cuts the Information wire.**
The actor no longer sees $\mathbf{\tilde{a}}$ as an input. But the $\beta$ penalty is *still active* in the loss function! So the actor is effectively blindfolded, yet still being mathematically forced to "do what the VLA would do." It has to painstakingly learn to guess the VLA's action from the state $\mathbf{x}$ alone just to satisfy the loss function.

**"w/o BC regularizer" cuts the Gravity wire.**
We set $\beta = 0$. The actor can still *see* $\mathbf{\tilde{a}}$ perfectly on the input. But the loss function no longer anchors the actor to it. The actor only cares about maximizing the Critic's Q-value. It is handed the VLA's suggestion on a silver platter, but has absolute mathematical freedom to ignore it.

If you look at the Ethernet ablation graph (Figure 7), dropping the BC regularizer ($\beta=0$) causes a massive, immediate drop in performance. But dropping the pass-through just slows learning down a bit. 

Based on how those two wires work, why do you think an actor that is blindfolded but constrained (w/o pass-through) eventually succeeds, while an actor that can see the reference but isn't constrained (w/o BC regularizer) fails so badly?

Remember your prior insight: you correctly pointed out that we *could* easily run the frozen VLA over the offline human demonstrations to generate the reference actions $\mathbf{\tilde{a}}$. So we aren't doing live warmup out of laziness or technical limitation. 

We are doing it to save the Critic from a classic deep RL delusion.

Imagine the offline Imitation Learning (IL) data as a perfectly smooth, straight path to the goal. If the Critic only ever trains on that perfect human data, it becomes an expert at valuing states exactly on the center line. 

But what happens the moment the live RL actor makes a tiny mistake—say, drifting two millimeters to the left?

*   The Critic is suddenly looking at an out-of-distribution (OOD) state. 
*   Neural networks don't fail gracefully on OOD inputs; they hallucinate. The Critic will often output a wildly optimistic, falsely high Q-value for that mistake.
*   The Actor follows the gradient of the Critic, sees this high Q-value, and actively steers *into* the error.

When we do a **live warmup**, we let the base VLA try the task. The base VLA is decent, but imperfect. It hesitates, it misaligns slightly, it wobbles. 

By feeding those specific "wobbles" into the replay buffer before online RL starts, what exactly is the Critic learning to do mathematically that it couldn't learn from the perfect human data?

You are exactly right that the fundamental mathematical requirement is just a mix of successes and failures. If you had a hard drive full of old, offline rollouts of the robot attempting the task and failing, that would absolutely work. 

But notice a subtle distinction: it needs to be the *VLA's* failures, not *human* failures.

Think about the shape of the state space. The volume of "all possible ways to fail" is massively, infinitely large. If you try to provide offline failures by having a human intentionally mess up, the Critic only learns to penalize human-like mistakes.

But neural networks don't fail the way humans fail. The VLA will drift off the path in its own weird, algorithmic ways. 

If the Critic hasn't seen the *specific* quirky states the VLA naturally wanders into, it will likely hallucinate a falsely high Q-value for them. Then, the moment the online RL Actor (which is anchored to the VLA) starts exploring, it will see those high Q-values and eagerly steer the robot right off the cliff.

So the warmup doesn't technically have to be "live" if you already have the data saved. But it *does* have to be generated by the base policy. You are using the VLA to scout the exact perimeter of its own ignorance, so the Critic knows exactly which nearby shadows to penalize.

Let's look at the two parallel universes from state $s_1$ (the arm swerving toward the table):

**Universe 1 (What actually happened):** 
The human inputs $a_1^{\text{human}}$. The human perfectly recovers, drives to the goal, and eventually gets the $+1$ sparse reward. Because Q-values flow backward, $Q(s_1, a_1^{\text{human}})$ is high (let's say $0.8$).

**Universe 2 (The Off-Policy calculation):**
The math completely ignores the human. It asks the *Actor* what it would do from $s_1$. The Actor says, "I would keep driving into the table." That trajectory fails, getting $0$ reward at the end. So $Q(s_1, \mathbf{a}_{\text{next}}) = 0$.

If the math used the human's reality, the target for the robot's bad move at $t_0$ would be $0 + \gamma(0.8)$. The Actor would get a massive gradient update telling it: *"Swerving into the table was a genius move! It led to success!"* (This is the reward leakage).

By forcing the equation to evaluate the *Actor's* next move, the target becomes $0 + \gamma(0) = 0$. The robot's bad move is correctly assigned a value of 0. That is the firewall. It stops the human's future $+1$ from bleeding backward into the robot's bad past.
